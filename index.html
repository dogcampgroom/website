Trim Nails

You should trim your dog’s nails whenever they are long. If they keep getting snagged or “click” on the floor as your dog walks, you know its time to get the clippers. Long unkempt nails can become ingrown or cause serious infections.

Shampoo and Rinse

You never can tell if a dog will like the bath; some dogs leap joyfully in, while other despise the bath. However, if you work to introduce your dog slowly to the water, offering some friendly rubs and verbal encouragement, your dog may learn to enjoy bath time.

Clean Ears

Cleaning your dog’s ears is important for keeping ear infections and wax build up at bay. Excessive wax and discharge create a favorable environment for bacteria and yeast to grow, which can lead to infection. For example, ear mites feed on the wax and oil in a dog’s ear. They don’t bite the skin but they do cause your dog to scratch his ears, and that can cause serious damage from his claws.

Dry and Brush Fur

Before every dog bath, you should make sure you give your dog a good brush-down. Again, grooming long-haired dogs will take longer, and need to be brushed more often; this is especially true if you want to avoid the hair from collecting around your home.

Double Coated Breeds

To complete this step you may choose to use dog-grooming sheers or a regular pair of hair-cutting scissors. Regardless what you use, just make sure you are extra careful with a sharp blade around the dog’s skin.

Remove Any Mats or Tangles

As you work with the dog to calm and reassure them, look for areas with matted or tangled hair. Before starting any shampoo you might find it helpful to apply a detangle solution to help remove those tangled or matted areas. Mats are even harder to remove when they come in contact with water, so you want to make sure that you remove the mats properly.
